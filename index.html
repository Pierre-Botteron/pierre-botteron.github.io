My PhD subject

Here are some keywords describing my subject:

  • Nonlocal games (e.g. CHSH game),
  • Nonlocal boxes (e.g. PR box),
  • Post-quantum strategies,
  • Communication complexity (it can be whether "trivial" or "non-trivial"),
Communication complexity is a way to measure how difficult it is for two distant players Alice and Bob to solve a problem together, given the right to send each other a limited amount of bits [Yao79, KN96].

In the Computer Science community, there is a strong belief that communication complexity could not be "trivial" in real life, in some sense meaning that there is no free lunch. Matching with that intuition, we already know that even if Alice and Bob use a quantum ressource to help them solving the problem, their communication complexity remains non-trivial (not surprising since quantum ressources exist in Nature...). On the contrary, we know that there exist some post-quantum ressources that collapse communication complexity and make it to be trivial... [brief historical overview]

We call the latter "collapsing boxes", and we try to study all the other post-quantum ressources, to see whether or not they are collapsing boxes as well (open question). If so, then our intuition of Nature would yield that post-quantum correlations are unlikely to exist, and that quantum ressources are the most general ressources existing in Nature...

My supervisors

The supervising team is composed in three complementary parts in Quantum Information Theory:
  • Anne Broadbent (University of Ottawa), expert in quantum cryptography, quantum nonlocality and quantum complexity theory.
  • Ion Nechita (CNRS, University of Toulouse), expert in random matrix theory, with a recent focus on the theory of random tensors, and in the theory of quantum entanglement.
  • Clément Pellegrini (University of Toulouse), expert on stochastic aspects of quantum mechanics, with a focus on the theory of quantum trajectories and open quantum systems.
